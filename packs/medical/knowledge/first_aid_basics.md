# Basic First Aid Guide

## IMPORTANT DISCLAIMER
This information is for educational purposes and emergency situations where professional medical care is not available. Always seek professional medical care when possible.

---

## Bleeding Control

### Minor Cuts and Scrapes
1. Wash hands before treating (if possible)
2. Clean wound with clean water
3. Apply gentle pressure with clean cloth
4. Apply bandage once bleeding stops
5. Watch for signs of infection (increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus)

### Severe Bleeding
1. Call for emergency help if available
2. Apply firm, direct pressure with clean cloth
3. Do NOT remove cloth if blood soaks through - add more on top
4. If possible, elevate injured limb above heart level
5. Apply pressure continuously for at least 15 minutes
6. If bleeding from arm or leg won't stop, apply tourniquet as last resort (above wound, not on joint)

**WARNING SIGNS - Seek immediate help:**
- Blood spurting or won't stop after 15 minutes pressure
- Deep wound or visible bone/tissue
- Object embedded in wound
- Wound from animal bite
- Signs of shock (pale, cold, rapid breathing)

## Burns

### Severity Assessment
- **First degree**: Red, painful, no blisters (like sunburn)
- **Second degree**: Blisters, very painful, red/splotchy
- **Third degree**: White or charred, may not be painful (nerve damage)

### First Degree Burns
1. Cool with running water for 10-20 minutes
2. Do NOT use ice (causes more damage)
3. Apply aloe vera or moisturizer after cooling
4. Cover loosely if needed
5. Over-the-counter pain relief if available

### Second Degree Burns
1. Cool with running water for 20 minutes
2. Do NOT break blisters
3. Cover loosely with clean, non-stick bandage
4. Seek medical care - infection risk is high

### Third Degree Burns
1. Do NOT immerse in water
2. Cover loosely with clean cloth
3. Treat for shock (keep warm, elevate legs if no spinal injury suspected)
4. **URGENT: Seek emergency medical care**

**WARNING SIGNS - Emergency:**
- Burns on face, hands, feet, genitals, or over joints
- Burns that go all around a limb
- Difficulty breathing (possible airway burn)
- Burns larger than palm of hand (in adults)
- Any third-degree burn

## Choking

### Adult/Child (over 1 year)
**If person CAN cough/speak:**
- Encourage coughing
- Do NOT slap back (can push object deeper)

**If person CANNOT cough/speak/breathe:**
1. Stand behind person
2. Make fist with one hand, place thumb side against belly (above navel, below ribs)
3. Grasp fist with other hand
4. Give quick upward thrusts
5. Repeat until object comes out or person becomes unconscious

**If person becomes unconscious:**
1. Lower to ground
2. Begin CPR if trained
3. Check mouth for visible object before breaths

### Infant (under 1 year)
1. Hold infant face-down on forearm, supporting head
2. Give 5 firm back blows between shoulder blades
3. Turn infant face-up, give 5 chest thrusts (two fingers, center of chest)
4. Repeat until object comes out
5. If unconscious, begin infant CPR

## Fractures and Sprains

### Signs of Fracture
- Severe pain that worsens with movement
- Swelling and bruising
- Visible deformity
- Unable to bear weight or use limb
- Grinding sensation

### Signs of Sprain
- Pain and tenderness
- Swelling
- Bruising
- Limited movement
- Possible "pop" sound at time of injury

### Treatment for Both
1. Rest - stop using injured area
2. Ice - apply cold pack (wrapped in cloth) for 20 minutes at a time
3. Compression - wrap with elastic bandage (not too tight)
4. Elevation - raise above heart level when possible

### Immobilization for Suspected Fracture
- Do NOT try to straighten the bone
- Immobilize joints above and below injury
- Use padding for comfort
- Use splint (board, rolled newspaper, etc.) if moving person is necessary

**WARNING SIGNS - Emergency:**
- Bone visible through skin
- Severe deformity
- No pulse below injury
- Numbness or tingling
- Blue/pale skin below injury
- Head, neck, or spine injury suspected

## Heat-Related Illness

### Heat Exhaustion
**Symptoms:** Heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, fast weak pulse, nausea, fainting

**Treatment:**
1. Move to cool place
2. Lie down, elevate legs
3. Apply cool wet cloths
4. Sip water slowly
5. If vomiting, seek medical help

### Heat Stroke (EMERGENCY)
**Symptoms:** High body temperature (103Â°F+), hot/red/dry skin, rapid strong pulse, confusion, unconsciousness

**Treatment:**
1. Call for emergency help
2. Move to cool place
3. Cool rapidly - immerse in cool water if possible, or apply cold cloths to neck, armpits, groin
4. Do NOT give fluids if unconscious
5. Monitor breathing

## Dehydration

### Signs
- Thirst
- Dry mouth and tongue
- Dark yellow urine or reduced urination
- Fatigue
- Dizziness
- Confusion (severe)

### Treatment
1. Give small sips of water frequently
2. If available, oral rehydration solution (ORS) is better than plain water
3. Avoid caffeine and alcohol
4. Rest in cool area

### Homemade Oral Rehydration Solution
- 1 liter clean water
- 6 teaspoons sugar
- 1/2 teaspoon salt
- Mix thoroughly until dissolved

**WARNING SIGNS - Seek medical help:**
- Unable to keep fluids down
- Bloody or black stool
- No urination for 8+ hours
- Severe confusion or lethargy
- Rapid heartbeat
- Sunken eyes

## When to Seek Professional Medical Care

Always seek professional medical care for:
- Difficulty breathing
- Chest pain
- Severe bleeding that won't stop
- Head injuries with confusion or unconsciousness
- Suspected broken bones
- Severe burns
- Signs of stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty)
- Allergic reactions with breathing difficulty
- High fever with stiff neck
- Pregnancy complications
- Any condition that is worsening despite treatment

This guide provides emergency information when professional care is not available. It is not a substitute for proper medical training or professional healthcare.
